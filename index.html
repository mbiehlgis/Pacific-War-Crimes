Horrors in the Far East

Preamble: One of the greatest failures in education is the outright neglection of history, especially history that continues to heavily impact the world today. A quintessential case of this can be found in how the American education system's learning material on World War II is disproportionately Euro-centric with stark ignorance of how the war raged on in the other half of the world. The periods before and between Pearl Harbor and the atomic bombing of Hiroshima goes entirely untouched. To put things into perspective, the death rate for Allied POWs captured by Germany was approximately 4%, whereas the death rate for Allies captured by the Japanese was a staggering 27%. The inherent issue with the ignorance of facts like this is that American students are not learning the events and the causes behind them that lead to horrific scenes in the far east, which could in turn pave a path for the world to repeat those mistakes. The interactive is also not representative of every occurrence of a Japanese war crime, but only a small sample of them for the sake of starting a conversation about the issue. Nor is this site designed to portray Japan as the sole Pacific Theatre party to have commit such acts of inhumanity.

Singapore

Alexandra Hospital Massacre

Alexandra Hospital


14 February 1942: On this Saturday afternoon on the grounds of a British Military Hospital Site, invading Japanese forces encroached on the facility after pushing through British defenses. The hospital itself had already faced heavy artillery throughout the morning along with internally poor conditions such as low food and water rations, scarce lighting, an overcapacity of patients, and an inability to properly bury the deceased. When the 1st Japanese soldiers were visible to the hospital guards, a Red Cross field worker was sent to discuss terms with the Japanese, however he was fired at upon first site despite his attempts to point out his Red Cross affiliation. The Japanese forces, under the command of General Tomoyuki Yamashita, attacked the hospital for the next hour going room by room executing, beating, and bayonetting all personnel as well as patients. A projected 50 people were killed in this first wave of attacks. Afterwards, the remaining 300 victims were forced on a march away from the hospital back towards Japanese controlled territory, those who fell or were unable to continue walking were executed.


Once the remaining prisoners arrived at the temporary Japanese base, they were separated into groups of approximately 70-80 and locked in 3 small rooms with hot temperatures and no access to water. As some died overnight to their previous wounds, the following morning some POWs were let out with the promise of water. However, shortly after they left the room, those that remained report hearing screams out in the distance, indicating that they were in fact being executed. Before the rest were killed however, the camp was struck by artillery, distracting the Japanese forces allowing for the escape of some of the POWs. In the end, a total of roughly 150 British POWs along with a handful of local hospital staff were killed in the massacre.

Indonesia

Laha Airfield Massacre

February 1942: Soon after the Battle of Ambon where Japanese forces took what is now present-day Maluku, Indonesia; an estimated 200+ Australian and Dutch POWs were summarily executed and buried in a mass grave. During prosecutions for war criminals after the war, prosecutor Major Alex Mackay told the court "The Australians were killed in a spirit of revenge." What he inferred was that this specific event was Japanese retaliation for the deaths of 20 Japanese navy crew members after their vessel struck a mine. Survivors of the attack were in fact a part of the squad that executed the 200 men.

Bangka Island Massacre

Group photo of nurses before the massacre

February 12th - February 16th 1942: On the eve of the fall of Singapore to Japanese forces, a royal yacht carrying wounded Australian servicemen, nurses, as well as women and children attempted to escape but was sunk at sea East of Indonesia. Around 100 survivors managed to swim to the nearby Bangka Island, not knowing the island was already under Japanese control. The officer in command believed the best chance at survival for the survivors was to surrender themselves to the nearest Japanese encampment. After returning with around 20 Japanese soldiers, all servicemen capable of walking were ordered to walk away with the officers. A repeated series of gunshots rang out before the 20 Japanese soldiers returned. Upon their return, reports released in 2019 revealed that the soldiers tortured and raped the nurses before ordering them to tread out into the sea where all but one would be executed by machine gun fire. Once the machine gun fire stopped, the soldiers returned to bayonet the remaining wounded servicemen on the beach. The only surviving members were Australian Army nurse Sister Vivian Bullwinkle along with 2 wounded servicemen.

SS Behar Massacre

March 1944: In early 1944, the Imperial Japanese Navy(IJN) set forth upon a series of naval raids on Allied supply ships under the command of Naomasa Sakonju to try and hinder ground forces already on land. On March 9th, the IJN struck and sunk the British merchant ship SS Behar, but not before approximately 104 of the Behar's crew abandoned ship. (It should be noted that attacking a neutral ship is considered a war crime under the Geneva Convention, capturing and unharming them however is not). Soon after, the Japanese warship 'Tone' went out to collect the crew that jumped overboard and subsequently inmprison them. However, this set of events went against Sakonju's initial orders to capture the ship and abandon any non-essential personnel. As a result, the Behar sent out a distress signal to other Allied ships, forcing the Japanese to retreat from their mission to the Netherlands East Indies. After 2 days, Sakonju ordered the commanding officer of the Tone, Haruo Mayzumi, to execute the 104 British crewmembers to try and cover up the attack. Mayazumi however felt strongly against the killings as he was a practicing Christian, and even requested Naomasa twice to give the prisoner's mercy. Sakonju was sharp in his convictions, and ordered Mayazumi to obey. That night, Mayazumi sent 15-34 disputed) of the Behar's officers and the only 2 surviving women to the Japanese ship 'Aoba' to be taken ashore as they would be spared and sent to a POW camp. The remaining 69 men however were executed via beheading by all of the Tone's officers except for Mayazumi who only watched from the ship's bridge, refusing to take part in the murder.

After the war ended, the Allies prosecuted the Japanese officer's involved in the crime. Sakonju was tried and convicted in Hong Kong by the British and sentenced to death. Mayazumi however was only sentenced to 7 years in prison for his role.

SS Tjisalak

March 1944: Another massacre at sea in relation to Japan's 1944 naval campaign to raid enemy supply ships was the attack on Dutch merchant ship "SS Tjisilak". While travelling from Melbourne, Australia to Colombo, Sri Lanka with a mix of Chinese, Dutch, and English seamen plus a handful of passengers and defensive gunners, the SS Tjisilak came into contact with the Japanese submarine I-8. At 5:45 am, the I-8 struck and sunk the Dutch ship with a torpedo, killing one crewmate instantly and sending the rest to abandon ship and escape to the lifeboats. The I-8 then emerged from the water and began capturing the some 105 surviving members. While the commanding Dutch officer was being interrogated by the I-8's commander, a Chinese crewmate slipped into the water and was shot instantly, with the I-8 crew believing an escape was attempted. In turn, the I-8 crew tied the rest of crew in pairs and began to beat them with their weapons. 4 of the men jumped into the water to escape the torture and miraculously managed to escape despite the subsequent gunfire at them. After the Japanese had killed about 20 of the members, they bound the rest together with rope and forced them overboard, drowning the rest of the crew.

The I-8 would then go on to strike the US Liberty ship the "SS Jean Nicolet", where a similar set of events occurred once more.

China

Unit 731

Group Photo of Unit 731 Officers and Scientists

March 1944: Based in the Pingfang district of Harbin in Northeast China was the headquarters for what is infamously referred to as "Unit 731". What began as a routine research and public health agency for Japanese occupied Chinese territory. Over time however, it evolved into a facility designed to study and weaponize a variety of diseases as well as the study of how much the human body can endure. The way this was accomplished however was via the torture and exploitation of local Chinese residents and allied POWs.

Biological Warfare: One of the primary methods of studying potential biological weapons was the deliberate breeding of plague-infected fleas and the subsequent aerial release of them over Chinese communities. These mass tests resulted in the deaths numbering in the hundreds of thousands. A potential 12 large-scale field tests were attacked by Unit 731's biological agents. Some other diseases released to the Chinese populace were Cholera, smallpox, and botulism.

Weapons Testing: Along with the myriad of biological tests that went on was the use of prisoners as live test dummies for the Japanese military's weapons to study their effectiveness on the human bodies. Among the weapons used were bayonets, grenades and other explosives, rifles, and even flamethrowers.


Maruta Experiments: One of the most prominent of projects that was orchestrated under Unit 731 went by the code-name Maruta. In this project, test subjects were gathered from the surrounding communities covering a variety of demographics such as criminals, anti-Japanese resistance members, the homeless, the mentally ill, and anyone the Kempeitai Secret police rounded up for "suspicious activities". These test subjects were then deliberately injected with deadly diseases under the guise of it being a vaccination in order to derive the most accurate of data. Along with the direct injection of diseases was the forced intercourse between subjects in order to spread and study venereal diseases, namely syphilis.

The Rape of Nanking

December 1937 - January 1938: Potentially considered the most infamous of Japanese war crimes is commonly referred to as "The Rape of Nanking", which took place after the Imperial Japanese Army captured the Chinese capital of Nanjing. During this 6-week period, upwards of 250,000 innocent Chinese citizens were tortured, raped, and killed by occupying Japanese soldiers. Being that the event took place over such an extended period of time, a myriad of various crimes and slaughters took place. A common theme found throughout all of the atrocities however is the absolute lack of humanity from the officers and soldiers present.

IJA Officers Toshiaki Mukai and Tsuyoshi Noda

Massacre Contest: One instance during this horror was the purported "massacre contest" that took place between two Japanese officers by the names of Toshiaki Mukai and Tsuyoshi Noda. The details of the "contest" they took part in entailed a race to see which of the 2 kill 100 civilians first with a katana. After this initial "round", they realized they had gotten too blood-lusted and lost count, so they began another round a few minutes later only the goal had now been raised to 150 people. The crime was originally published and framed by Japanese media as a "heroic killing of the Chinese". After the war, Mukai and Noda were both detained, prosecuted, and executed for their crimes.

Mass Rape: Along with the outright massacre of Chinese civilians were the mass raping that occurred every day and night of the 6 weeks. Japanese soldiers were given free reign to systematically travel door-to-door to capture, rape, and murder any women, as well as young girls and boys, over the course of this horrific event. There were also multiple cases of soldiers forcing family members to commit sexual acts upon one another. It wasn't uncommon for the soldiers to mutilate the victims' genitalia with their bayonets or broken shards of glass after assaulting them. Pregnant women also were not spared from these heinous actions.

Lingering Effects: The scale and scope of this atrocity still plays a huge role in Sino-Japanese relations, where some Japanese ultra-nationalists claim the event to have been highly exaggerated, namely due to how the IJA quickly tried expunging any and evidence of the crime before Japan's surrender in 1945. It took until August 15th, 1995, the 50th anniversary of Japan's surrender for Prime Minister Tomiichi Murayama to give Japan's first formal apology to the people of China. Despite the presence of a large business and trade relationship between the two nations today, many Chinese citizens are still distrustful of the Japanese due to their hesitance to admitting their guilt.

The Philippines

Manila Massacre

February 1945 - March 1945: As Americans campaigned closer and closer to the Japanese homeland, the Philippine islands were slowly but surely being reclaimed by allied forces. The overseer of all IJA activity in the country, General Tomoyuki Yamashita, even requested an immediate withdrawal of his forces from Manila in January to prevent the now dire situation he and his men faced, but this order was ignored by his subordinate Rear Admiral Sanji Iwabuchi, who chose to stay along with his 10,000 men. However his forces were quickly outgunned and outmatched by the growing American military presence in the city; his marines in turn became increasingly angered and hostile, often taking out there frustrations on Filipino civilians in the brief respites between battle. By the time American marines regained full control of the capital, nearly 100,000 Filipino civilians had been killed by the Japanese.


A Deceased Manila Woman and her Child


The crimes these soldiers inflicted upon these Manila civilians were eerily reminiscent of those committed in Nanking 8 years earlier. Thousands of men, women, and children were tortured and butchered, often with bayonets. Along with the killings were several mass rapes of women and girls as young as 12 by the Japanese. During the trials of the officers responsible for the killings, it was revealed that the local Bayview Hotel was designated by the Japanese as a 'Rape Center', where some 25 local Filipino girls were abducted from their homes and kept in the building for officers to repeatedly rape.


IJA General Tomoyuki Yamashita

Filipino Civilian Woman


Despite these atrocities taking place under the command of Rear Admiral Iwabuchi, it was General Yamashita who was convicted and put on trial for the war crimes that took place. However, there existed no evidence that Yamashita committed any crimes, ordered them to happen, or had the capability to stop them. Regardless, General MacArthur and 5 other generals chose to hold him accountable since he was in command of all Japanese troops at the time. A group of American military lawyers tried to defend Yamashita by appealing to the Supreme Court who ultimately rejected the appeal 5-2. Yamashita was executed by hanging February 23rd, 1946 in Manila.

Bataan Death March

April 1942: After 3 months of battle between American and Filipino soldiers under the command of General Douglass MacArthur, and the invading Japanese under the command of General Masaharu Homma, the Bataan Peninsula finally fell in early April of 1942, a much sooner surrender than the Japanese expected. An unexpected result of the relatively quick surrender of the peninsula was the massive underestimation of expected POWs which ranged from 60,000-80,000 American and Filipino troops. General Homma was then forced with the daunting logistics task of transporting tens of thousands of men to the prison camp North of the peninsula, a staggering 65 miles away. While Homma was busy devising a plan to move the prisoners, a forced stripping of material possessions from the men, however any men who were found to have any Japanese items or money on them were executed for allegedly looting Japanese corpses.


Captured American and Filipino soldiers being
lined up to be sorted for the march


Homma soon realized the only option he had to get these POWs out of the area before beginning his next assault was to have them physically march there. He then gave the order to have his subordinate officers facilitate this move, which he ordered to have done peacefully. His serving officer Colonel Masanobu Tsuji chose to go against his wishes and gave private orders of his own to other officers encouraging them to summarily execute their respective sets of captives. Due to these orders, an estimated 400-500 Filipino officers were soon gathered along the Pantingan River and executed via gunshots, bayonets, as well as beheadings.

Victims of the march


Eventually the march was in motion, where the POWs faced intense heat, exhaustion, starvation, and drought all under the threat of being killed for falling out of line. It should be noted that many men had already been wounded and fatigued from the months of battle preceding the march. A common form of torture used on the men was referred to as "Sun Treatment", where men were forced to sit in the blistering heat without any forms of shade for hours on end, some placed just in sight of fresh water. Anyone who asked for water however was immediately shot. Some men who simply could not walk any longer collapsed on the road where they would either be bayonetted, left for dead, or ran over by passing Japanese trucks. Guards would also randomly stab prisoners with bayonets for no reason at all.



When the first part of the march ended at San Fernando railhead, the men were divided into hundreds and forced into unventilated box cars which only had a capacity of 40. Inside the box cars on the way to the next destination, the men now faced a rapid spread of dysentery and disease. They eventually reached Capas train station where they would face the final 9 miles of their march. Despite reaching the prison camp, the rate of deaths continued at several hundred per day due to fatigue and disease. Of the original 80,000 Americans and Filipinos that started the march, only 54,000 remained. Of the deaths, an estimated 18,000 were Filipino and 600 were American, with several men still unaccounted for.

Palawan Massacre

14 December 1944: In mid-August of 1942, 300 American POWs captured during the Battle of Bataan were shipped to a slave work camp in Palawan, an elongated island Southeast of Manila. For the next 2 years, the imprisoned Americans would be forced to perform slave labor for the Japanese clearing and building an 8-inch thick 1500 yard long runway to resupply and refuel Japanese war planes. As Japan lost more of its control over the course of the war, rations for prisoners became growingly scarce, resulting in most POWs receiving just 1 bowl of rice per day. In September of 1944, half of the original 300 POWs were sent back to Manila to be redesignated elsewhere in the Japanese supply line.


The trench the men built and died in

One month later, the airfield the men constructed began to face multiple Allied bombing raids that destroyed a notable amount of Japanese planes and ship. While rejoiced at the renewed hope of being freed, the situation at hand forced the men to build trenches to take cover in. In December however, American forces were on the brink of defeating the Japanese controlling the camp's area of the island. To prevent the rescue of the men, the commanding officers purposefully sounded off the air raid alarm indicating for the men to get into the trenches they built. Japanese soldiers then surrounded the trench and proceeded to douse it and the prisoners and gasoline before setting them on fire. Prisoners who managed to escape the flames were shot while those burning inside were stabbed with bayonets. Just 11 men managed to escape their deaths.



Site of the Massacre

The US discovered the remains in early 1945. The massacre triggered a string of POW rescue campaigns by the US to prevent future atrocities from taking place. After the war, survivors from the massacre assisted in pointing out Japanese officers and guards in charge and present at the massacre who were subsequently tried for their crimes. Today, 123 of the victims are now buried in a mass grave at Jefferson Barracks National Cemetery in St. Louis, Missouri.

Shinyo Maru Massacre

7 September 1944: The SS Shinyo Maru was a Japanese merchant ship conscripted by the IJN to be turned into a transport ship for POWs. Ships of this nature were dubbed as "Hell Ships", due to the fact that the POWs on board would not only face hellish torture from their captors, but also frequently be at risk of being killed by Allied torpedoes and planes. In the incident regarding the Shinyo Maru, about 750 Allied POWs (300 of which were Bataan Death March survivors) were present on board when the USS Paddle, an American submarine, launched 4 torpedoes at the ship with 2 of them making target. The explosions killed several men both Japanese and American. The Japanese officer in charge of the POWs then ordered his men to open fire on the prisoners with submachine guns to deny them any chance of rescue. While some men were able to escape the gunfire and dive into the water, many were subsequently killed by the depth charges the Shinyo Maru crew dropped as a final attempt to kill them off. Of the original 750 POWs, 668 were killed and only 82 survived the chaos.

South Korea

Korean Comfort Women

"Women's Column of Strength"
A statue in San Francisco in Memorial
to WWII Comfort Women in the Pacific

1932 - 1945: A common practice by the Japanese military was the construction of official brothels where soldiers would be allowed to release any sexual urges on the female hosts at these sites. These women however were very likely to have been abducted and forced into this prostitution, many of them starting before the age of 18. Despite the Korean peninsula being annexed as a Japanese colony in 1910, these brothels would not start their construction until 1932.


Korean and Chinese women being forced into brothel

While the program began in 1932, it was not until the massacre and mass rape of Nanking in 1937 that the brothels became to be widespread. After the atrocity in China, Emperor Hirohito worried that irreparable damage could be done to Japan's image if similar events took place. As a result, the brothels started to appear throughout the empire to reduce the frequency of such atrocities by isolating the rapes to only a carefully select few women and girls. Another reason for their widespread creation was for the prevention of widespread venereal diseases among soldiers, which could have weakened Japan's total strength over time. While the women they abducted were paid for their services, it is still at this time historically disputed as to how these women were treated. Survivors from the victimized countries demand for Japan to make an official apology about the issue, but some members in Japanese parliament claim there is little direct evidence that the women were treated with malice. While an official apology was made in 1994 with 364 surviving comfort women from various countries were issued an official signed apology from then Japanese prime minister Tomiichi Murayama along with monetary compensation. The 61 Korean women however claimed the apology was unofficial since the funds were made via private donations instead of the Japanese government itself. The women would settle the issue when the compensation reached $42,000 USD per woman.

Photo of South Korean citizens protesting for an
offical apology to be made from Japanese PM Shinzo Abe

Tensions rose again in 2007 when current Prime Minister of Japan disputed claims for an official apology to be made as no direct evidence existed of any malice or severe mistreatment of the comfort women and that they were compensated fairly at time. Abe would eventually rescind his statement with an official apology in a settlement with South Korean president Park Geun-hye. Along with the apology was $8.3 million USD to a fund supporting surviving women, in exchange the South Korean government agreed to cease any criticism of Japan about the issue as well as to remove a statue memorializing the event previously placed in front of the Japanese embassy in South Korea.

Malaysia

Parit Sulong Massacre

Site of Bridge Ambush and Massacre

23 January 1942: During the Battle of Muar in Johor, Malaysia, Allied members from India and Australia were making a strategic retreat after ambushing and blowing up a Japanese supply bridge. Before everyone could retreat however, the Japanese managed to surround and pin down the remaining men; healthy soldiers were told to scatter and escape into the jungle and head back to allied territory. However some 150-300 men were too wounded to make any attempt at escape and were forced to lay down their arms and surrender. The Japanese they faced however were still very much angered at the loss of their bridge, and were not interested in taking in any POWs. As retaliation, the Japanese guards beat the men with their rifles while tying them together with industrial wire. The guards would then stand the entrapped men in the middle of the street where they would either let loose machine gun fire on them or douse them in gasoline and burn them alive. In other cases, the groups of men were forced to stand on the remains of the bridge they ambushed. Here the Japanese would shoot and kill just one of the men so that while their body fell into the river below, the momentum would subsequently pull in the rest of his group causing them to drown. Only 2 men would survive the massacre, but their survival allowed them to make sure the massacre was known and for the perpetrators to receive justice.


Lt. Ben Hackney of the Australian Army
One of the 2 survivors of the massacre


The commanding Japanese officer responsible for the massacre was Lieutenant General Takuma Nishimura, who was also overseer for the Sook Ching massacres in Singapore. After the war, he was tried for his crimes in Singapore by a British military court where he received a life sentence, of which he only served 4 years. On his way back to Japan after his release however, he was apprehended in Hong Kong by Australian military police to be tried for his crimes at Parit Sulong. He was convicted and executed via hanging on June 11th, 1951.

Sook Ching Purge

18 February - 4 March 1942: After the surrender of the British in their colonies of Singapore and Malaya in 1942, the invading Japanese forces set forth on a campaign to purge any suspected anti-Japanese Chinese citizens residing in the newly conquered territory. The systemic purge was carried out by the Imperial Japanese Army's secret police dubbed the "Kenpeitai". Under the Kenpeitai's rule, several mass executions and tortures were carried out by their auxiliary enforcement units named the "Hojo Kenpei", however the 3 most notable and infamous were located at Punggol Point, Changi Beach, and Belakang Mati Beach. Over the course of Japan's occupation of the area, over 30,000 suspected Chinese residents were executed.

Japanese Military Police executing Chinese citizens via firing squad


Changi Beach: On February 20th, 1942, the first of the Sook Ching mass killings took place. On this day, 66 Chinese male citizens were rounded up and marched to the Changi Beach shoreline and executed via firing squad. Along Changi's main road, a suspected 250 more victims were executed in a separate massacre after the discovery of a mass grave after the war ended. It would mark the beginning of a harsh and brutal occupation for Singapore's Chinese community.

Punggol Point: During this event, around 1,000 Chinese residents in the Upper Serangoon neighborhood were forcibly taken from their homes by the Hojo Kenpei. While nearly 600 were either killed when resisting capture, another 400 were marched to the Punggol Point shoreline and executed via firing squad. In the decades following the massacre, residents and fishermen who frequented the beach would occasionally discover human bone fragments and even a skull in one instance.

Belakang Mati Beach: In this event, 300 captured British soldiers awaited their transport to Japanese internment camps. While waiting along the shoreline however, human bodies exhibiting multiple bullet wounds began washing up due to a mass firing squad execution that took place on a Japanese Naval Vessel. The British were then forced to bury the remains as they awaited their internment.

Micronesia

Wake Island Massacre

Aerial View of Wake Island

5 October 1943: The Battle and occupation of Wake Island has solidified itself in history as one of the most iconic locations in the Pacific, in part due to the island's natural horseshoe like shape. Originally an American military airbase, the island was attacked simultaneously with the bombing of Pearl Harbor, where US personnel would hold out until December 23rd with scarce resources and defenses. The valiant defense just after Pearl Harbor however proved to be a slight morale boost to Americans at home. The Japanese would hold the island for the duration of war in the Pacific, despite the fact that US forces had already advanced and captured islands beyond its location. Instead of embarking on an amphibious assault to retake the island, the US Navy opted instead to host a submarine blockade against any Japanese resupply attempts along with a series of bombing raids. The blockade was successful in that it starved the occupying Japanese for several years, but failed in the sense that it could not force the Japanese into submission. The severity of one of these raids struck fear into Japanese Rear Admiral Shigematsu Sakaibara, so much so that he believed an invasion was imminent. This train of thought ultimately persuaded him into ordering the executions of all 98 remaining American POWs. The execution itself involved the men being blindfolded on the beach and killed by machine gun fire.


Wake Island Airfield


Just one of the men managed to escape the slaughter and hide in the islands treeline, at some point however he returned to the massacre site where he discovered the mass grave his fellow soldiers were buried in. Before leaving, the unknown soldier inscribed on a boulder "98 US PW 5-10-43" to provide evidence regarding what happened that day. Eventually the soldier was recaptured by the Japanese, and was executed via beheading by Sakaibara himself with his katana. When Japan ultimately surrendered in 1945, Sakaibara ordered for the exhumation of the mass grave and for the bodies to be buried at the official US gravesite on the island as a means to cover up the crime. When interrogated about how the 98 Americans were killed, he ordered his men to claim that many were killed by Allied bombing raids and the rest were killed in self-defense for attempting a revolt. Many Japanese officers beneath Sakaibara were left very disturbed by the incident, with some committing suicide when the Americans retook the island and had them in custody. Before they died however, they left incriminating statements describing what happened that day and how Sakaibara was directly responsible. As a result, Sakaibara was convicted and sentenced to death by hanging in Guam.


"98 Rock"


The remains of the 98 men were eventually exhumed once more to be moved and buried at their final resting place in National Memorial Cemetary of the Pacific in Honolulu.

Guam

Faha and Tinta Massacres

The Village of Merizo in Guam

15 July 1944: In the summer of 1944 on the island of Guam, the Japanese occupiers stationed and maintaining control over the island began to feel intense frustration over the increasing likelihood of being defeated by the American Marines who were closing in on the island. In their anxiety and frustration over their probable surrenders or death, the Japanese soldiers took out their aggression on the local population of the Merizo village. On the night of the 15th, the soldiers went into the city demanding for 30 men and women to march to the caves near the local foothills under the guise of being put to work. As soon as the villagers all entered down the cave entrance however, the Japanese began tossing grenades into the enclosed area, killing 16 of the victims. In their subsequent search for survivors to execute however, they failed to notice the remaining 14 using the corpses of other now dead villagers as shields against bayonet stabs; ultimately allowing for them all to survive until the island's liberation a few weeks later.

Memorial Service for the Victims of Both Massacres

After learning of the 14 villagers' survival the following day, the Japanese became even more frustrated at the situation they were in. As a result, the soldiers again rounded up another 30 men and women in Merizo, this time selecting only prominent members of the community to be killed. To ensure the success of the execution however, the soldiers marched the selected villagers to an area known as Faha where they would line them up to have grenades thrown directly at them as opposed to in a cave. Those that survived the initial grenades were then personally butchered with bayonets. No one would survive this second slaughter.

A Plaque Commemorating the Massacres at
Tinta and Faha outside of Merizo

When the people of Merizo discovered what had actually happened to the 60 people that had been taken away, they became enraged. 4 days later on July 20th, 1944, a large group of men from Merizo stormed the local Japanese headquarters and killed any Japanese soldier in sight, only 1 soldier would survive their vengeful onslaught. Today the horrific events and sites are of great historical significance to the Guamanian people as it portrayed both hardship they endured and the courage they found in the face of monstrous evil.

Bonin Islands

Chichijima Massacre

September 1944: The island of Chichijima was a tiny Japanese-controlled military asset only 700 miles South of Tokyo. In late 1944, the island was raided by a US bomber squadron in a strike mission; anti-air artillery however managed to shoot down a bomber flying overhead forcing the craft's 9-member crew to escape. 8 of the men who jumped parachuted to the island's surface where they would immediately be captured by the Japanese, they would all experience horrific torture and daily beatings as POWs. All 8 of the men would eventually be executed in a variety of gruesome manners such as beheading, impalement, or being beaten to death. At least 4 of the men's bodies however would not even find piece in death, as the Japanese officers on the island at the time ordered for their bodies to be prepared and eaten as part of a feast for the officers. The supposed reason behind this act of cannibalism was both for believed spiritual benefits as well as to show an act of dominance and vengeance to the other POWs for their bombing of the island. The ninth and only surviving member of the crew was the only soldier fortunate enough to escape this fate as he landed his parachute in the water where nearby American gunships would protect him with covering fire and provide him with a life raft. That man would turn out to be 41st US President George H.W. Bush (pictured above).
